Fractions

Fractions are a way to divide whole objects into smaller chunks. We do this every day. When you order a large pizza, they cut it up into 8 pieces. Each piece is 1⁄8 of the pizza. If you eat 3 slices, you've eaten 1⁄8 of the pizza. If you have 2 pencils and give 1 pencil to your friend, you now have half (1⁄2) the number of pencils that you started with.

There are infinite ways to write the same fraction, though, so it can get a little tricky. For example, 3⁄6 is the same as 1⁄2. Luckily, you're already a wiz at multiplication and division, and a fraction is just a funny looking division problem.

What is a Fraction?

A fraction is made up of a Numerator and Denominator. The numerator is the top number, and the Denominator is the bottom number. All division problems are fractions! So when you've done 12 ÷ 3, you're doing 12⁄3. However, in that fraction, you can simplify the fraction down to a Whole Number. Remember, a whole number is a number like 1 or 7 or 68943. It doesn't have any decimals or fractions attached to it.

The fractions you'll be practicing here, however, won't come out to whole numbers. If you want more practice with that, visit the division section.

Consider This:
We use fractions for everything. Think about your height. We know that there are 12 inches in a foot. So if you're 4 feet 2 inches, what they really mean is that you're 4 2⁄12 feet tall!
You can also simplify/reduce the fraction to 4 1⁄6 feet, or complicate/expand the fraction to 50⁄12 feet!

Try it!
Ask a partner to measure your height with a tape measurer. Then, try to simplify/reduce and complicate/expand your height! For extra fun, try telling your math teacher your expanded height and see if they can guess it!

How To Find Equivalent Fractions.

Let's find the Equivalent of 3/5 when the denominator in the incomplete fraction is 30.
3 / 5 = ? / 30
  • Start by taking the two denominators, 3 and 30
  • Divide the bigger number by the smaller number (30 ÷ 5 = 6)
  • Now, multiply the numerator by the number we just found, 6, to get your missing numerator.
  • 3 x 6 = 18
  • The answer is 3⁄5 = 18⁄30

So we know how to find the answer, but why is it the answer? Imagine cutting up a pizza into 5 slices and giving away 3 of those slices. Then you cut up another pizza into 30 pieces (that's a lot of pizza slicing!) and give away 18 of them. The two pizzas still have the exact same amount left, even though one is cut up into smaller slices than the other.

But Wait! Theres More!

The same approach works when the denominator is missing. Instead of comparing the denominators, you compare the numerators.

Let's find the Equivalent of 3/5 when the numerator in the incomplete fraction is 9.
3 / 5 = 9 / ?
  • Start by taking the two numerators, 3 and 9
  • Divide the bigger number by the smaller number (9 ÷ 3 = 3)
  • Now, multiply the denominator by the number we just found, 3, to get your missing denominator.
  • 5 x 3 = 15
  • The answer is 3⁄5 = 9⁄15

Conclusion: There are many different ways to write equivalents, but a little division and multiplication makes it very easy!

How To Simplify/Reduce Fractions.

Simplifying fractions is also pretty easy. Just figure out if numerator and denominator can be divided by the same number while still being a whole number. Let's walk through it.

Let's simplify this fraction.
9 / 12
  • Start by seeing if the numerator can be divided by anything, other than 1.
  • 9 can be divided by 3 and 9 and produce a whole number.
  • Now, see if the denominator can be divided by any of those numbers.
  • 12 can't be divided by 9, but it can be divided by 3!
  • Divide both the numerator and denominator by this common number to get your answer.
  • 9⁄12 simplified/reduced is 3⁄4
  • Double Check: perform the steps again using 3⁄4. Since 3 can only be divided by 3, and 4 cannot be divided by 3, you have simplified the equation.
Let's try a different one.
8 / 12
  • Start by seeing if the numerator can be divided by anything, other than 1.
  • 8 can be divided by 2, 4 and 8 and produce a whole number.
  • Now, see if the denominator can be divided by any of those numbers.
  • Wait! 12 can be divided by both 2 and 4!
  • Divide both the numerator and denominator by the larger number, 4, to get your answer.
  • 8⁄12 simplified/reduced is 2⁄3
  • Double Check: perform the steps agains using 2⁄3. Since 3 cannot be divided by 2, you have simplified the equation.

But what if I accidently picked 2 instead of 4?

Don't worry! That's why we double check. If you had picked 2 instead of 4, your answer would have been 4⁄6. 4 can be divided by 2 and 4. 6 can be divided by 2 as well. Since there is still a shared factor, you haven't simplified the equation yet. Instead of redoing the whole problem, you can just do 4 ÷ 2 and 6 ÷ 2 to get 2⁄3. Don't forget to double check again, just to be sure!

Another Example.
7 / 11
  • Start by seeing if the numerator can be divided by anything, other than 1.
  • 7 can be divided by 7 and produce a whole number.
  • But 11 can't be divided by 7...
  • 7⁄11 is already a simplified fraction. You can't do anything to it!
A BIG Example.
336 / 504

This one seems hard! What if we break up the fraction rather than finding the one factor they both have in common? This approach is similiar to if you had picked 2 instead of 4 in the second example. You don't have to start by dividing by the biggest number, especially when you have no idea what that number is. Start by dividing by something you can tell they have in common.

  • Both 336 and 504 are even numbers, so we know they can divided by 2.
  • 336 ÷ 2 = 168 and 504 ÷ 2 = 252.
  • We're in luck! Both of these are divisible by 2 as well! Let's do it again.
  • 168 ÷ 2 = 84 and 252 ÷ 2 = 126.
  • Again, two more even numbers, so divide by two AGAIN .
  • 84 ÷ 2 = 42 and 126 ÷ 2 = 63
  • Hmmm, 63 is an odd number, so it can't be divided by two. It ends in 3 though, so we know its a multiple of 3.
  • Yay! 42 can also be divided by 3!
  • 42 ÷ 3 = 14 and 63 ÷ 3 = 21
  • Now we're getting somewhere! Both 14 and 21 are products of 7.
  • 14 ÷ 7 = 2 and 21 ÷ 7 = 3
  • Can you believe it? You just simplified 336/504 to 2/3!

That was a lot of work! So if we saw that exact same expression next time, what would we divide it by? Just multiply all the numbers you divided by!

  • 2 x 2 x 2 x 7 x 3 = 168.
  • 336 ÷ 168 = 2 and 504 ÷ 168 = 3
Improper Fractions and Mixed Numbers

Let's talk more about pizza. If you have a whole pizza and cut it up into 8 slices and don't move the slices, you have 8⁄8 of a pizza. We know 8 ÷ 8 = 1, which makes sense because you have 1 whole pizza. If you take away one slice, you now have 7⁄8 of a pizza, which is less than a whole. But what if someone told you they had 16⁄8 of pizza? If there are only 8 slices of pizza, how can you have 16 slices? The only thing that makes sense is if you have two pizzas!

An Improper Fraction is a fraction whose number on top is bigger than on bottom, like 16⁄8 of pizza. What this means is there is more than one whole pizza being counted in that fraction. Another example, if you make a dozen (12) cookies and your friend makes a dozen cookies, you now have 24/12 cookies. A dozen is 12, so 12 makes up 1 whole batch of cookies. You have 2 batches of cookies, so you have 24/12 cookies.

So how do we deal with improper fractions? Let me show you. Let's say you ordered 2 pizzas and they were cut into 8 slices each (16 slices). But you ate 1 slice, so now you have 7 slices in one pizza (7⁄8) and 8 slices in the other (8⁄8) for a total of 15⁄8 slices.

Pizza Time!
15 / 8
  • Keep in mind before we start: 8 slices is one whole object because it takes 8 slices of pizza to make 1 whole pizza.
  • 15 is greater than 8, so we know we have at least one whole object. Let's remove that whole object and set it to the side.
  • 15 - 8 = 7 (1 whole removed)
  • Can you take out another whole object (8)? No. So now we have 1 whole object and 7⁄8 of an object
  • The mixed number is 1 7⁄8 because it has an integer/whole number (1) and a proper fraction(1 7⁄8)
Cookies Galore!

Let's try a harder one. Let's say you made 40 cookies for your classmates and teachers. A dozen (12 cookies) makes up one whole batch. How many batches did you make?

40 / 12
  • Take out as many whole batches as you can. 40 - 12 = 28, 28 - 12 = 16, 16 - 12 = 4.
  • We were able to take out 3 batches of cookies because we subtracted a whole batch (12) 3 times.
  • So now we have 3 and 4⁄12 left. The 4⁄12 is there because after subtracting 3 batches, we still had 4 cookies left over.
  • So we made 3 4⁄12 batches of cookies.
  • But wait! 4⁄12 can be simplified!
  • The mixed number would be 3 1⁄3 batches of cookies.
  • Bonus: Why is this called a mixed number?
How Tall are You?

Ask your parents to measure you in inches (not feet!). Then, try to figure out how tall you are in feet without looking at the measuring tape. For this example, we'll use someone 62" tall.

40 / 12
  • We know 12 inches = 1 foot. So 1 whole foot is 12 inches, or 12⁄12
  • Someone who is 62 inches is 62⁄12 feet tall.
  • You know 12 x 5 = 60 because you're a wiz at multiplication!
  • Let's take out 5 whole feet, since 12inches (a whole foot) goes into 62 inches 5 times.
  • Now we have 5 feet and 2⁄12 of a foot.
  • A proper fraction would be 5 1⁄6 feet (but we'd never say that!)
  • In America, we would just say we are 5ft 2in and everyone would know that 2 inches is 2⁄12
